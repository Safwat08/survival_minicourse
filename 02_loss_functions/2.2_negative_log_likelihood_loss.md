# Negative Log-Likelihood Loss

Negative log-likelihood (NLL) is a general fitting principle. This lesson covers two
common survival versions: **fully parametric** models, which choose a distribution
for event time, and **discrete-time** models, which assign probability across time
bins. Cox also minimizes a negative log-likelihood, but of a *partial* likelihood;
see [`2.1`](2.1_cox_ph_loss.md).

## The likelihood

Parametric models are fit by **maximum likelihood**: choose the parameters that make the data you actually observed most probable. The *likelihood* is that probability, read as a function of the model's parameters.

Each subject $i$ (with observed time $T_i$ and event indicator $\delta_i$ — 1 = event seen, 0 = censored; the `(T, δ)` notation from [`1.2`](../01_background/1.2_censored_data.md)) contributes **one factor**, depending on what we saw:

- **Event observed** ($\delta_i = 1$): we know it happened at $T_i$ → contribute the density $f(T_i \mid x_i)$.
- **Censored** ($\delta_i = 0$): we only know they lasted past $T_i$ → contribute the survival probability $S(T_i \mid x_i)$.

Assuming subjects are independent, the **full likelihood** is the product over everyone:

$$
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \prod_i \; f(T_i \mid x_i)^{\,\delta_i}\; S(T_i \mid x_i)^{\,1-\delta_i}
$$

The exponents $\delta_i$ and $1-\delta_i$ are a switch: each subject's factor is *either* $f$ (event) *or* $S$ (censored), never both — one exponent is always 0, and anything to the power 0 is 1.

## From likelihood to loss

Two standard moves turn that product into a trainable loss — the **same two steps** Cox applies to its partial likelihood in [`2.1`](2.1_cox_ph_loss.md#the-loss):

1. **Take the log → the *log-likelihood*.** The log of a product is a sum of logs, so $\log \mathcal{L} = \sum_i \ell_i$. The log is monotonic (it doesn't move the maximizer), it turns an underflow-prone product of tiny probabilities into a stable sum, and it yields cleaner gradients.
2. **Negate → the *negative* log-likelihood.** Optimizers minimize, so flip the sign: maximizing $\log \mathcal{L}$ is the same as minimizing $-\log \mathcal{L}$. That negative is the loss, **NLL**.

The per-subject log-likelihood term and the total loss:

$$
\ell_i \;=\;
\underbrace{\delta_i \,\log f(T_i \mid x_i)}_{\text{event seen: density at } T_i}
\;+\;
\underbrace{(1-\delta_i)\,\log S(T_i \mid x_i)}_{\text{censored: survived past } T_i}
$$

$$
L \;=\; -\sum_i \ell_i \quad\text{(the negative log-likelihood, summed over all subjects)}
$$

> **The recipe is the same as Cox.** Both losses are *negative log-likelihoods of a product*: write the likelihood as a product → take the log to get a sum → negate to get a loss. The only difference is **which** product. Cox uses a *partial* likelihood — one softmax factor per event, built so the baseline hazard cancels ([`2.1`](2.1_cox_ph_loss.md#partial-likelihood)). NLL uses the *full* likelihood — one $f$-or-$S$ factor per subject, which is why it needs a distribution but, in return, predicts absolute probabilities.

Spelling out the two functions, both derived from the chosen distribution:

- **$f(t \mid x_i)$** — the **probability density function (PDF)**. In continuous time, $f(t)$ is a density rather than the probability of one exact time (that point probability is zero); probabilities come from integrating the density over an interval. An observed event contributes the density evaluated at its recorded time.
- **$S(t \mid x_i)$** — the **survival function**: the probability of surviving *beyond* $t$, i.e. $S(t) = P(T > t)$. Used when the subject is **censored**, because all we know is that the event hadn't happened yet by $T_i$.

> **Why the split?** An observed event gives you a *point* on the time axis — score it with the density $f$. A censored subject gives you only a *lower bound* ("at least this long") — score it with the survival probability $S$ of having lasted that long. Using $S$ for censored subjects is how parametric models extract information from incomplete follow-up instead of discarding it.

> **Worked example — two subjects.**
> - Patient A has the event at $T_A = 8$ months ($\delta_A = 1$): contributes $\log f(8 \mid x_A)$ — "how likely was an event right at month 8?"
> - Patient B is censored at $T_B = 12$ months ($\delta_B = 0$): contributes $\log S(12 \mid x_B)$ — "how likely was surviving past month 12?"
>
> The total loss is $-[\log f(8\mid x_A) + \log S(12 \mid x_B)]$.

## Common distributions

The choice of distribution fixes the **shape the hazard is allowed to take** over time. Pick the one whose flexibility matches your domain.

| Distribution | Hazard shape it allows | Pick it when… |
|---|---|---|
| **Exponential** | Constant (flat) | Risk doesn't change with time — "memoryless." Rarely realistic, but the simplest baseline. |
| **Weibull** | Monotone — steadily rising **or** falling | Risk only ever increases (e.g. aging/wear) or only ever decreases (e.g. early post-op recovery). Most common parametric default. |
| **Log-normal** | Non-monotone — rises then falls | Risk peaks at some time then declines (e.g. a complication that's most likely mid-follow-up). |
| **Log-logistic** | Can be unimodal or decreasing, with **closed-form** $S(t)$ | Useful when a hump-shaped or decreasing hazard and a closed-form survival function are plausible. |

> **What "monotone hazard" means.** *Monotone* = moves in one direction only. A
> Weibull hazard either climbs the whole time or falls the whole time; it cannot peak
> and reverse. Log-normal hazards are unimodal, while log-logistic hazards can be
> unimodal or decreasing depending on their shape parameter.

## In deep learning

A neural network supplies the distribution instead of a fixed-coefficient model. There are **two distinct flavors** — the [Implementation](#implementation) section gives code for each:

**1. Parametric — predict distribution parameters.** The network maps each patient $x$ to the *parameters* of a chosen distribution (e.g. a Weibull's shape $k$ and scale $\lambda$), so every patient gets their own curve. Plug those into the closed-form $f$ and $S$ above and minimize the NLL.

- *"Parameterize the distribution"* means exactly this: the net's outputs **are** the distribution's parameters, as a function of the input.
- Example model: **Deep Survival Machines (DSM)** — predicts a *mixture* of parametric distributions (see [`2.4`](2.4_deephit_loss.md) and [`04_packages/4.5_auton_survival.md`](../04_packages/4.5_auton_survival.md)).

**2. Discrete-time — predict a hazard per time bin.** The continuous time axis is chopped into bins, and the network predicts the conditional event probability (a discrete hazard) in each bin. The NLL is then a sum of per-bin Bernoulli terms. This sidesteps choosing a parametric family at all.

- Example models: **Nnet-survival** (Gensheimer & Narasimhan, 2019), pycox's **LogisticHazard**, and **MTLR**.

> **Note — Nnet-survival is a discrete-time model, not a parametric one.** It predicts a hazard rate per follow-up interval; it does *not* fit a Weibull/log-normal shape. It belongs to flavor 2 above. (It's a real, published model — [PeerJ 2019](https://peerj.com/articles/6257/), [repo](https://github.com/MGensheimer/nnet-survival).)

## Key takeaways for applied ML scientists

If you just need to train and use an NLL model — not derive it — these are the points that matter:

- **Output includes absolute probabilities.** A fitted parametric or discrete-time NLL model predicts a full survival curve $S(t \mid x)$. Unlike a bare Cox risk score, it can directly answer a fixed-horizon probability question—subject to calibration and validation.
- **You owe one modeling decision Cox doesn't ask for.** Either **pick a distribution** (parametric flavor — Weibull, log-normal, …) or **bin the time axis** (discrete flavor). Choose the *parametric* flavor when you trust a hazard shape and want smooth extrapolation; choose *discrete-time* when you'd rather not commit to a shape and have enough data to fit per-bin hazards.
- **Parametric models can extrapolate; discrete ones stop at their grid.** A fitted Weibull formula continues past the last observed event, but that mathematical ability is not evidence that the extrapolation is accurate. Long-horizon use needs strong subject-matter justification and sensitivity analyses.
- **Evaluate calibration, not just discrimination.** Because the output is a probability, the C-index alone isn't enough — also check the **Brier score** and a **calibration curve** (do 70%-survival predictions actually survive ~70% of the time?). A model can rank well yet be badly calibrated.
- **The distributional assumption is the risk.** A wrong family biases the whole $S(t)$. Sanity-check the fit against a non-parametric [Kaplan–Meier](../01_background/1.1_what_is_survival_analysis.md) curve before trusting the numbers; if no standard shape fits, switch to the discrete flavor or [DeepHit](2.4_deephit_loss.md).
- **Censoring is handled by the loss itself.** You don't drop or impute censored subjects — they enter through the $S(T_i)$ term automatically. Just pass `(times, events)` correctly.

For how NLL stacks up against the other losses, see [`2.5`](2.5_loss_comparison.md).

## Implementation

The two flavors map to two kinds of API call.

**torchsurv** — parametric Weibull AFT loss for a custom model; the network predicts log-shape and log-scale (see [`04_packages/4.3_torchsurv.md`](../04_packages/4.3_torchsurv.md)):

```python
from torchsurv.loss.weibull import neg_log_likelihood_weibull

log_params = model(x)             # network predicts log shape & log scale per subject
loss = neg_log_likelihood_weibull(log_params, events, times)
```

**pycox** — discrete-time NLL models; the continuous time axis is binned first via a label transform (see [`04_packages/4.1_pycox.md`](../04_packages/4.1_pycox.md)):

```python
import pycox.models as models
# LogisticHazard / PMF / MTLR all minimise a discrete-time NLL
labtrans = models.LogisticHazard.label_transform(num_durations=20)
y_train = labtrans.fit_transform(durations, events)
model = models.LogisticHazard(net, optimizer, duration_index=labtrans.cuts)
```

**lifelines** — classical parametric fitters, one per distribution (see [`04_packages/4.2_lifelines.md`](../04_packages/4.2_lifelines.md)):

```python
from lifelines import WeibullFitter, LogNormalFitter, WeibullAFTFitter
WeibullFitter().fit(T, E)                          # univariate
WeibullAFTFitter().fit(df, "T", "E")               # with covariates
```

> **Parametric vs. Cox.** Parametric NLL gives a full predicted $S(t)$ and a
> formula that can extend beyond observed follow-up, at the cost of a distributional
> assumption Cox avoids. Cox can also produce $S(t)$ after estimating its baseline,
> but does not parametrically extrapolate that baseline. For a side-by-side, see
> [`2.5`](2.5_loss_comparison.md).
