# What is Survival Analysis?

Survival analysis models the time until an event of interest occurs. Unlike standard regression, the outcome is not just *whether* the event happened, but *when*.

## Key Concepts

- **Survival function S(t)**: Probability of surviving beyond time *t* — P(T > t)
- **Hazard function h(t)**: Instantaneous rate of the event at time *t*, given survival up to *t*
- **Cumulative hazard H(t)**: Integral of the hazard over time

## Why Not Standard Regression?

Standard regression cannot handle:
- **Censored observations** — subjects who haven't experienced the event yet
- **Time-varying risk** — hazard may change over time

## Estimating These Functions

You rarely compute S(t) and H(t) by hand. They can be estimated directly from the data — *non-parametrically*, meaning without assuming any equation for their shape. The two standard estimators come from `lifelines` (see [`references/6.2_lifelines.md`](../references/6.2_lifelines.md)):

```python
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter, NelsonAalenFitter

KaplanMeierFitter().fit(T, E)      # estimates S(t)  — the survival function
NelsonAalenFitter().fit(T, E)      # estimates H(t)  — the cumulative hazard
```

Here `T` is each subject's observed time and `E` is the event indicator — the `(T, δ)` notation introduced in [`1.2`](1.2_censored_data.md). The **Kaplan-Meier** estimator is the workhorse of survival analysis; it gets a full treatment in [`5.3`](../05_metrics/5.3_km_curves.md).

These are the *descriptive* baseline — they summarise a whole group. Linking individual patient features (**covariates**) to risk is the job of the models in [`03_loss_functions/`](../03_loss_functions/3.1_cox_ph_loss.md).
