# What is Survival Analysis?

Survival analysis models the time until an event of interest occurs. Unlike standard regression, the outcome is not just *whether* the event happened, but *when*.

## Key Concepts

- **Survival function S(t)**: Probability of surviving beyond time *t* — P(T > t)
- **Hazard function h(t)**: Instantaneous rate of the event at time *t*, given survival up to *t*
- **Cumulative hazard H(t)**: Integral of the hazard over time

### Hazard vs. cumulative hazard

These two are easy to confuse. The difference is *rate at an instant* versus *total accumulated so far*:

- **Hazard h(t)** is a **speedometer**: given you've survived up to *t*, how fast is the event happening *right now*, at this exact moment? It is a rate (events per unit time), not a probability — it can even exceed 1.
- **Cumulative hazard H(t)** is an **odometer**: how much total risk has piled up between time 0 and time *t*? It is the running total of all those instantaneous rates.

They are tied together by integration, and the survival function follows directly:

$$H(t) = \int_0^t h(u)\,du \qquad\qquad S(t) = e^{-H(t)}$$

(Odometer = integral of the speedometer.)

## Why Not Standard Regression?

Standard regression cannot handle:
- **Censored observations** — subjects who haven't experienced the event yet
- **Time-varying risk** — the hazard may change over time. The instantaneous rate h(t) need not be constant: after major surgery, for example, the hazard of death is high in the first days (complications), then drops steeply as the patient recovers. Standard regression predicts a single fixed number and cannot say "risk is high now but low later"; survival analysis models the whole h(t) curve.

## Estimating These Functions

You rarely compute S(t) and H(t) by hand. They can be estimated directly from the data — *non-parametrically*, meaning without assuming any equation for their shape. The two standard estimators come from `lifelines` (see [`04_packages/4.2_lifelines.md`](../04_packages/4.2_lifelines.md)):

```python
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter, NelsonAalenFitter

KaplanMeierFitter().fit(T, E)      # estimates S(t)  — the survival function
NelsonAalenFitter().fit(T, E)      # estimates H(t)  — the cumulative hazard
```

Here `T` is each subject's observed time and `E` is the event indicator — the `(T, δ)` notation introduced in [`1.2`](1.2_censored_data.md). The **Kaplan-Meier** estimator is the workhorse of survival analysis; it gets a full treatment in [`3.3`](../03_metrics/3.3_km_curves.md).
