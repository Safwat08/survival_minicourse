# Time-Dependent AUC

Standard ROC-AUC applies to a fixed binary outcome. **Time-dependent AUC** extends it to survival data by re-defining who counts as a "case" and a "control" *at each time point* $t$ — turning a single number into a curve over time. Where the [C-index](3.1_c_index.md) gives one global discrimination score, this shows *when* the model discriminates well.

## Definition

At a chosen time $t$, split patients into cases and controls, then ask the same concordance question as the C-index:

$$
\text{AUC}(t) \;=\; P\big(\,
\underbrace{r_i > r_j}_{\text{model scores case riskier}}
\;\big|\;
\underbrace{i \text{ is a case at } t}_{\text{has had the event}},\;
\underbrace{j \text{ is a control at } t}_{\text{still event-free at } t}
\,\big)
$$

where $r_i$ is the risk score. The split itself comes in two flavors:

- **Cumulative/Dynamic** (most common): **cases** = everyone who had the event by $t$ ($T_i \le t$, $\delta_i = 1$); **controls** = everyone still event-free at $t$ ($T_j > t$).
- **Incident/Dynamic:** **cases** = only those whose event falls in a narrow window *around* $t$ ($T_i \in [t-\Delta, t]$); controls as above. Useful for "how well does the model flag risk *right now*?"

> **Worked example — AUC at $t = 12$ (cumulative/dynamic).** Five patients, evaluated at month 12:
> | Patient | Time $T$ | Event | Role at $t=12$ | Risk $r$ |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | A | 6 | 1 | case (failed by 12) | 0.9 |
> | B | 9 | 1 | case (failed by 12) | 0.5 |
> | C | 18 | 1 | control (event-free at 12) | 0.4 |
> | D | 20 | 0 | control (event-free at 12) | 0.2 |
>
> Compare each case against each control (4 case–control pairs); count those where the case is scored riskier:
> - A(0.9) > C(0.4) ✓, A(0.9) > D(0.2) ✓, B(0.5) > C(0.4) ✓, B(0.5) > D(0.2) ✓ → 4/4
>
> $\text{AUC}(12) = 1.0$ — at this horizon the model perfectly separates those who have failed from those still at risk. Computing it at $t = 8$ instead (only A is a case) would probe a different, earlier horizon.

## Mean AUC

A weighted average of $\text{AUC}(t)$ over a range of times, using the event density as weights, collapses the curve into a single summary comparable to the C-index:

$$
\overline{\text{AUC}} \;=\; \frac{\int_{t_{\min}}^{t_{\max}} \text{AUC}(t)\, w(t)\, dt}{\int_{t_{\min}}^{t_{\max}} w(t)\, dt}
$$

## Key takeaways for applied ML scientists

- **Use it when the time horizon matters.** If a clinical decision is made at "2 years," report $\text{AUC}$(2 yr) directly instead of a horizon-agnostic C-index. The horizon you pick here is the natural one for the [Youden threshold](3.3_km_curves.md#choosing-a-risk-threshold) cutoff.
- **It diagnoses *when* a model is good.** A flat-high AUC curve is robust; one that sags at long horizons tells you predictions degrade far out — something a single C-index hides.
- **Cumulative/Dynamic is the default**; reach for Incident/Dynamic only when you specifically care about risk *in a window* rather than cumulative risk.
- **Same IPCW time-window caveat as Brier** (below) — evaluation times must stay inside observed follow-up.

## Implementation

**scikit-survival** — cumulative/dynamic AUC over a vector of time horizons (see [`04_packages/4.4_scikit_survival.md`](../04_packages/4.4_scikit_survival.md)):

```python
from sksurv.metrics import cumulative_dynamic_auc
import numpy as np

times = np.percentile(time_test[event_test], [25, 50, 75])     # within follow-up range
auc, mean_auc = cumulative_dynamic_auc(y_train, y_test, risk_scores, times)
```

**torchsurv** — AUC as a PyTorch metric for in-loop, time-resolved monitoring (see [`04_packages/4.3_torchsurv.md`](../04_packages/4.3_torchsurv.md)):

```python
from torchsurv.metrics.auc import Auc
auc = Auc()(log_hz, events, times, new_time=eval_times)
```

> **Same time-window caveat as Brier.** Both rely on IPCW censoring weights, so evaluation `times` must stay inside the observed follow-up. The time horizon you pick here is also the natural one for the [`03_metrics/3.3_km_curves.md#choosing-a-risk-threshold`](../03_metrics/3.3_km_curves.md#choosing-a-risk-threshold) cutoff.
